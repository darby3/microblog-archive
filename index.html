So I did the half marathon 🏃

May 22, 2024

So I did the half marathon!

First off—amazing experience. Considering I went from three weeks out thinking “I’m still injured, I’m not doing anything” to two weeks out thinking “Maybe I can walk it?” to one week out thinking “Well, I can run a little of it, at least,” what I wound up actually doing felt incredible.

My plan was fluxing pretty much up til race day, with the hard limit of knowing I couldn’t run the whole way—I had to stay conservative if I wanted to be sure I was going to make it to the finish line, coming off the leg injury that imploded my full marathon training. Where I pretty much wound up was, was that I wanted to run the first mile, and then I wanted to run the last two miles, and then I was going to alternate half-mile walks with running stretches for the remainder of the race, knowing of course that everything would depend on how I felt in the moment, and that the whole thing was likely to go up in smoke at a moment’s bad turn.

I wound up doing nearly the best possible version of that plan. Did the first mile, broke to walk, did another mile, broke to walk. Three miles in and was feeling good and so I started to stretch the runs and shorten the breaks, and I started feeling like I could live-adjust my distances to try to fit the water stations in to walking stretches. Somewhere in the middle I had a solid two mile stretch, that was definitely not in the plan. I had some surprisingly quick miles in there along the way. And I was just feeling good.

Coming toward the end, a big reason for wanting to run out the last two miles was because the mile 11 marker comes right before the Hope Memorial Bridge, the one with the Guardians of Traffic statues, which is about as iconic of a mile as I suspect you can find in Cleveland; I really didn’t want to miss that stretch. And then of course you’ve only got a mile left after that so I mean might as well run it out, right?

I also had it in the back of my mind that for as conservatively as I was running the rest of the race, if I got through mile 12, I’d give myself permission to do something possibly a little dumb that last mile. (As a little treat!) And I felt so good coming off the bridge that I felt okay kicking it up a gear for the last mile, until I was basically sprinting into the finish. My pace rose by around two full minutes over the course of the last two miles. I kind of thought I was going to die at the end and it was the most incredible feeling imaginable.

Overall the race was a great experience. The one other formal half marathon I’ve done was a couple Novembers ago, taking place on a very cold, very very cold, very very very cold day in the Metroparks, on a double-out-and-back path. I will always have a soft spot for that race and I hope to do it again in the future, but it was comparatively a much smaller experience, with less crowd support and a race crowd that probably skewed a bit more toward harder-core, faster runners, I think?

This race, however, with several thousand people out there on the course, was so energizing. While I didn’t know anybody I was running near I also never felt like I was alone, as I was constantly surrounded by other runners. Seeing lots of other folks doing intervals was reassuring as well. And the crowd support surrounding the course was so cool—there’s no doubt my pace picked up every time I passed through groups of onlookers. (I hope it was obvious when I was laughing at the signs, too, that it didn’t just look like I was making anguished faces!) It’s a cool experience to be part of something putting out that much positive energy into the world.

As for final race time—I didn’t have any goal, of course, other than to get to the finish line in one piece, but even with the walk breaks, I wound up coming in only about 6 minutes behind where I landed at my previous half marathon race. Which, I mean. Wow!?! Where did that even come from? I’ll take it.

There’s of course so many other little details that stick, of course. Of course there was the banana suit guy, somewhere out there. Of course I saw a much older-than-me woman well ahead of me running barefoot like it was no big deal. Of course I heard lots of poop jokes. (So many poop jokes.) Of course there was the guy standing on his front porch who, right as I was passing by, said, “Hey, y’all wanna slow down a bit, I’m about to twist one up.” Of course there’s finishing my half marathon run as the winners of the full marathon were rolling in. (I mean, my god. I’m still catching my breath from my finishing half mile.) Of course the beers at the finishing line party were the greatest things I’ve ever tasted in my entire life. And of course there were the congratulatory chalk drawings from my kids in the driveway when I got home. Of course and of course and of course.

Marathon plans 🏃

Feb 5, 2024

I’m technically three weeks into marathon training and I don’t think I’ve gone into any real detail yet on what my training plan or goals look like. Partially because the first three weeks have felt so “light” it’s hardly felt like I’ve been training for anything yet, and partially because I’ve yet to fully convince myself I actually am training.

The way I’m looking at this right now is, I’m definitely absolutely totally training for a marathon, but I haven’t fully committed to which one, yet. My current goal is to do the Cleveland Marathon in May; if I miss that, for whatever reason, I’ve got at least one local backup marathon, the Towpath Marathon, in October, I can fall back on. I’m sure there’s others I could target in there but I’m not ready to plan on traveling to an event yet.

My hesitancy here stems from how, coming out of the end of last year, I didn’t know if I’d have the get up and go to keep getting up and going outside in the dead of winter to start very quickly training again for a May race. This felt like a valid concern at a point when I was really looking forward to a break! After three weeks, though, for as much as I sure do want to burrow away and hibernate until April because it has sucked out there, I’ve still definitely actually been getting out there. So if nothing else, there’s reason for cautious optimism, there!

As for the plan I’m following, after looking at a bunch of first-timer options, all offering various blends of “go run a lot, and then run some more, and then keep running,” I’ve taken to the Hal Higdon Novice I plan as my starting point, because it seems both attainable and kind of interesting. On the one hand it feels attainable, like it’s just chill enough to help me just get to the finish line without worrying about things I don’t need to worry about this time, while on the other hand, the rhythm of the plan is interesting, compared to how I worked up to half marathon lengths in the fall. That round I was keeping my weekday runs all balanced, roughly the same length, plus or minus a mile, while the weekend long run was, of course, long. The Higdon plan packs more of the midweek mileage into one of those runs, so you’re kind of sandwiching a longer midweek run between two shorter runs. It feels a little funky and intriguing and I’m curious to see if I can make it work. That said, I’m reserving the right to balance those weekday as needed if I find myself losing consciousness during certain workdays.

Other mild modifications: I’m squeezing a cross-training day (light elliptical) into the week instead of doing that on the weekend, so I’ve been active Monday through Thursday, with rest days before and after the long run on Saturday, so I have at least one night a week when I can enjoy a beverage or two after we finally get the kids to bed and before I pass out on the couch. I’m also stepping things up just a skitch by introducing some informal speed work in during the week, because after a year of not doing that at all, I’ve felt like I’ve missed it. Nothing crazy, just an informal mile of fartlek intervals here, a little more steady pace running there. I’m staying conservative with it but it does feel nice to pull those other levers again.

As for strength training, I’m…opting not to freak out about it. It remains an inconsistent weakness of mine, but then, there’s just only so much time. I would like to get some body weight stuff again in somehow, but I haven’t figured out how to actually make that work with my schedule.

No pace or time goals for the race itself, beyond finishing, ideally without walking. I’m completely happy with knowing I’m a slow runner, of course—like, if I came in faster than five hours, I’d probably assume a timer malfunction. I do still hope to run the whole route, without walking, because while I know there is absolutely nothing wrong with walk-run methods or walking breaks, I’m still weirdly stubbornly committed to trying not to do that. It’s maybe my one version of upping the ante, or keeping the ante consistent, for myself.

As for when I’ll commit to a race, I’m chunking the 18 week plan into two pieces, with the goal right now of just getting to week 8, when I’ll be back up at the half marathon length again for the weekend long run. Once I hit that point, sometime in early March, if I’m feeling good, if I’m feeling strong, then I’ll feel better about putting money down for the May race. If not, October is, relatively speaking, just around the corner(!), but I’ll have plenty of time to reassess and plan accordingly. One way or another I do think that finish line is in my future.

Once upon a time I was a math guy.

Like, when I went to college, I was going to double major in English and Math, which is crazy to think about today. It did not go well! I did not make the transition from high school math to advanced math successfully, in part because it got real hard—nothing can quite prepares you for the part where math suddenly stops seeming to have much to do with numbers. I managed to escape college with a math minor, making way for a creative writing minor. Now it’s *mumbles unintelligently* years later and sure I still write, sort of, and sure some aspects of mathematical thinking inform my ability to make computers do things, but, like…I don’t know. I wonder how much college Darby would recognize adult-with-mortgage Darby. If the two would have much to talk about.

My dreams are far more idle these days.

Once in a while something tweaks and fidgets some old dormant strain of me and I catch myself wondering how much of those old pathways still exist, how much they dangle into empty but inviting space; if I picked up a calculus book, today, if I looked at the texts that came next, could I make heads or tails of any of it? I have no time for it, I have other things I can actually do in the little time I have, and yet some diluted flavor of curiosity lingers, even after all these years.

I also still think a bit about the notion from my one chaos theory class that you’ll eventually get everywhere when you run the same equation long enough and I suppose that would be more metaphorically informative today if I had an actual idea where I was trying to get to with this kind of line of thinking. And also I’ve probably got that whole concept wrong, because, well, yeah. Math got hard.

On (Not) Running

Jun 8, 2023

About seven weeks ago I dropped out of my half-marathon training around the midpoint of the twelve-week plan. After weeks of struggling through various pains, I’d had enough. It sucked. Going into training, I thought I was in a really good place. And then I wasn’t.

Seven weeks is about the longest I’ve taken off from running since 2021. That time I think I rage quit because I wasn’t having fun and I was running very poorly. That time I needed the mental break; this time I needed the physical break. I know both breaks were necessary. That first time I needed a hard reset. This time around, though, I mentally felt ready for anything, at a time when my body decided to give me the finger. I’ve been striving toward the point where consistent, regular running was a natural part of my routine, so I could alternate progressing up toward new goals and floating back down to a solid, easy base. Needless to say, it did not work out like that.

I’m not trying to dwell on any of this. I do want to put a bow on it as I start to move on. Several times over the last month or so I’ve sincerely toyed with the thought that maybe that was actually it, maybe I’m done now, maybe I just won’t go back.

ButI I’m going back. Just this week I started back up again at something like square one….one mile runs, that is! Which isn’t actually square one—I’m still light-years beyond where I was when I started running in 2019. I do not want to take that for granted. Still, as eager as I am mentally to get back to long-as-heck weekend runs, I know it’s going to be a while and I know how important it is to ease back up to where I actually want to be.

And, yes, even just getting out there for fifteen minutes last night felt nice, like a taste of something that I know has been missing the last few weeks. I haven’t been idle since I stopped training. I mean, yes, I took a hard week off at the start, and last week was a low one, and I’ve been a bit all over the place in the middle, but I have been trying to at least get up on the elliptical for some easier movement, and I’ve also been once again trying to get into something resembling a regular strength routine, specifically body weight exercises targeting my leg muscles in an effort to give my knees some support. Finding things I actually don’t mind doing has been a nice turn and I know it’s important now to figure out how to keep that stuff going as I start to slide back into running. Making the time for it sucks but then not doing anything sucks even more, so.

Aaaaaand of course today I started marking out races and potential training start dates on my calendar, so…no pressure, of course, but also, it’s nice to feel like I have those goals back in front of me, still.

We’ll see what happens.

My Big Dumb Running and Music Challenge of 2023 🎶 🏃

Jan 9, 2023

I track and share my runs on Strava; on a whim, on my second run this year, I posted, in that run’s notes, the album I listened to during the run (Strange Little Birds by Garbage.) I did the same thing for the third run (Womb by Purity Ring) and the fourth run (Endure by Special Interest). Once I figured out what I ran to for the first run of the year (The Physical World by Death From Above 1979) I went back and added that, too.

Somewhere along the line, during one of these slow, easy runs, I started wondering: can I go the whole year without listening to the same album twice across any runs?

This is a dumb idea. It probably adds more stress to my life than I need. How am I going to track it? How am I going to pick new stuff if I make it to, like, December, or, heck, June? What happens if I use a favorite album too soon, or ruin it on a bad run? Does this actually result in a cool diary of what my year of running sounds like, or does sharing the fact that I do in fact sometimes listen to Taylor Swift absolutely destroy me in the public eye?

Still, it’s the kind of dumb seed of idea that finds fertile ground to develop into a big goofy plant of an idea when I’m out running, when I’m trying my hardest not to think about anything, and now I’m here with a bunch of thoughts and contraints around this absolutely pointless challenge I’m now apparently setting out for myself this year:

  • I’ll continue sharing the album on Strava, and possibly do monthly or quarterly round-up posts here. Until I forget. And then I won’t.
  • Loophole: if I’m craving something truly embarassing, I can redact it from the public records. But, like, as impeccably cool as my first four choices have been, it’ll pretty quickly become clear that, like, my music taste is far from impeccably cool.
  • Once I start the album, that’s it, it’s on the list. Plenty of runs won’t last long enough to cover entire albums. That’s fine. Also the album might just not work and I might have to click over to something else mid-run (see loophole below); the album still goes on the list.
  • Loophole: I’m only going to count the first album of any run. So like on long runs if I queue up multiple albums, anything I listen to after album number one doesn’t count. I’m probably mostly a deliriously unreceptive slate by that point anyway, so it really shouldn’t count.
  • That said, double albums? No two-disc loophole: it’s all one album. (So, yes, I’ll be saving stuff like The Fragile by Nine Inch Nails for seriously long runs.)
  • What happens when I’m running stays when I’m running; anything I listen to outside of running doesn’t count. (Having only recently discovered them, I can safely say I’m going to listen to that Purity Ring album a couple dozen more times this year.)
  • Tracking is by album and not artist. There’s probably going to be a couple discographies I run through. (No pun intended.)
  • Throwing a “liked tracks” list on shuffle is perfectly valid at any point for any reason. And doesn’t count against the list.
  • Same deal for DJ mixes. I have a bucket of these bookmarked on Soundcloud. They’re fun. And they won’t count.
  • Loophole: races don’t count. Historically I haven’t listened to music during races. This year I think I might, and if I do, anything goes; after all, what better time to bring out the big guns?

It’s funny to think about how my relationship to music when I’m running has changed over the last 3.5 years, from my original need to carefully soundtrack every single minute of every single run with custom playlists (it was only mildly high-maintenance…), through to my current of attitude of listening to pretty much literally whatever so long as I like it. Not that I don’t still generally prefer more upbeat stuff when I go out; it’s unlikely I’m about to go out running to Selected Ambient Works Volume II by Aphex Twin anytime soon. But “upbeat”, it seems, can take on a lot of forms, when you have zero thoughts about pace matching or anything like that. (I’d be pretty curious to see what a two-hour run to Autechre’s NTS Session 4 would do to one’s headspace.)

2023 Running Plans 🏃

Nov 29, 2022

I’m planning on running a full marathon in 2024.

I’ve been planning for this since I started training for the half marathon I just ran. I looked at the half marathon as a chance to dip my toe in the distance-running water to see whether I liked the feel for this kind of training or whether it felt like too much. So far, so good: nothing about the half marathon cycle I just went through made me want to give up.

It’s funny, because even with what I’ve done, I still feel like I have a little imposter syndrome, when it comes to running. I still feel very conservative in my approach, using the word “training” still kind of makes me want to break out in hives, and I come into this knowing that running a full marathon isn’t some huge life-long dream of mine. (For that, you’d have to look at biking. While I’ve biked one metric century, I still feel like I’ve got at least one full 100 mile ride in me. Some day.) I started running three years ago in order to support a particular charity 5k, and with two kids in the house I was looking for a form of exercise that was a bit more effective than bicycling (which long rides, when I was training for that metric century, took me out of the house forrrevvvver), and because I generally thought it would be cool to be able to run a full mile without stopping. After a couple rocky years I got more consistent with running last year and started wondering if I could run a marathon.

And I started to think that maybe I could. And I started to think that maybe if I thought I could, if I even had an inkling of interest in it, maybe I should do something about it sooner rather than later, because while I’m not old, I’m certainly not getting any younger. At one point I thought about just going straight for it in 2023 but then decided I’m just not that hardcore. So I came up with a loose plan to give myself well over a year to build up to it while also giving myself plenty of freedom to back out at any time for any reason with no penalty.

And now here I am, my first half marathon in the books, and as of right now, that big-picture goal still seems reasonable. I mean, it seems crazy, but also possible. Nothing about this process the last few months made me want to quit. Whether or not I actually make it all the way I feel like I’m having fun right now, like I’m building up a sustainable running practice, and I’m learning a lot about what motivates me to keep going, and of which is worth it.

And I can still run one whole mile without stopping. And that still does feel pretty cool!

That said, I’ve learned a bit about how goal motivated I am. Races—as much as I anguish over them—bring structure and focus to all the runs that come before them. So I’m going to keep doing them, and I’m planning on doing two more half-marathons in 2023. I want to re-run the Fall Classic in November, because I want to have at least one year-over-year apples-to-apples comparison, just to see what there is to see. Before that I want to try for a late spring race, I think, because I’d like to run at least one of these races in (hopefully) nice weather. I’ve earned that!

I’m also ready to care about pace a little more. Admittedly, I ran the November race slow enough that it would be tough not to beat that time next time out, after another round of training, but also I just know I can go faster than that, and I want to specifically try for that. I never expect to be competitive at these things, nor am I interested in trying hard enough to be. But I also know it’s fun to surprise yourself, and I can get behind trying hard enough for that. So I’m setting out with the loose goal this spring of beating my first time, and then going for something reasonably ambitious in the fall.

To that end, in the short term, I want to run more during the next training cycle. Not necessarily harder, but more. I know I trained at about the barest possible minimal level for the fall race. Next time out I want to go from three days a week to four. I think I’m going to repeat the same basic 12-week training calendar as the last time out, which I liked the general feel of, with the addition of the fourth day, which would be a shorter, light/easy day. I have no plans to target five days as I want to keep a day or two free for an elliptical session or finding a strength training or yoga habit I like.

While I said I don’t necessarily want to run harder, I do generally want to bring my paces up across the board. Sometimes these last few months I felt fine, sometimes I felt like I had more to give but was holding back. I’m still not quite sure what easy should actually feel like but I think just in general the more I run the more efficient I get and if I can maintain my consistency through into the new year I’ll naturally feel a little more okay going a little more fast a little more often. Maybe more importantly though, for confidence purposes, I want to push my race pace experiments more aggressively. I want to pick an ambitious target early and stick with it the whole way through. I really wasn’t sure what felt right this last time through, and now I think I want to use those runs to push myself to give a little more. Still, even if it’s nothing like what I run on race day, having those faster days in the training cycle feels fun.

I’m also hoping to do some shorter races that I can really try for my best-possible paces. I’m thinking about re-doing the 10k I ran last spring, which left me feeling like I had something unresolved there that I’d like to make good on. I’m also curious to see if I can beat my summer 5k race’s time from last year, too. Which actually feels like it could be the most ambitious goal of the year! If I find some 10k races that fit in with the half marathon plans, I might try to slide those in, too, for practice purposes, but I’m not sure that’s going to work out or not.

So that’s the year in a nutshell, as I picture it right now: a half, some shorter stuff, and then a give-it-my-all half by the year’s end. Working on confidence, pace, and strength. And then all going well, launching into full marathon training come January 2024.

Week twelve running report ... race week! 🏃

Nov 28, 2022

Monday afternoon, about 24 hours after I crossed the finish line, is when I finally started to believe that I’d just run my first half marathon. I felt pretty spaced out before that, like I’d been walking through a post-race cloud, and it was only when I started to come out of it that I felt like I could not just know I did it as a fact but feel that I did it as a truth. If that makes sense. Like I could feel the nervous energy start to dissipate into a sense of accomplishment. It made for a nice post-race high.

That said: oh my lord, I have never before felt cold like I did right after that race.

To back up a bit: the week before the race I continued my taper. It got a little weird! I’d planned on Tuesday/Thursday runs like usual with super-easy Monday/Friday elliptical sessions, all basically just to keep loose while resting up. Monday went fine, then Tuesday fell apart for various reasons, including truly miserable weather, and I wound up cancelling. So then I went back out for a handful of miles Wednesday night, a few at “race pace,” then had to turn around and get up early on Friday to get one more run in before work and the weekend. It all worked out fine, and my fears of over-sleeping Friday came to nothing, but the whole stutter-step of a final week’s schedule was slightly nerve-wracking.

I felt good Friday, and slept like a baby that night, which was nice, because I knew I’d probably not sleep like a baby the night before the race. (Spoiler alert: I did not sleep good Saturday night.) Saturday felt like one of the longest days of my life. I’m learning just how susceptible I am to pre-race nerves. I know it’s healthy to a degree, the body’s way of getting amped up for the coming event. But I also think I was tipping a bit past “excitement” and more into “bro, you must chill” territory. I did not enjoy myself much that day. Again, everything worked out! But I know I want to work out a better day-before strategy for the future.

Leading up to race day I was watching the weather forecast. Specifically, I was watching the forecasted temperature drop like a rock. Signing up for a late November race in northeast Ohio, I have some idea what I’m getting myself into. But! Yikes! Based on my experience at the 5k at the same event last year, I was happily anticipating low-40s, gray clouds, maybe some fog. You know…perfect running weather. I was not prepared for the low 20s. With a wind chill that took it down to single-digit territory.

“How cold was it,” you ask? Could enough that I’d never adequately had the chance to test race day gear—I’d run in three layers of clothing earlier that week, but decided at the last minute to throw on a fourth vest layer on top on my running fleece layer. Cold enough that every time I thought I was warmed up enough during the run that I could unzip those top two layers to vent, I was zipping back up within minutes. Cold enough that the water they were giving out on the course was actually freezing—at least, there were definitely ice chips in mine. Cold enough that a friend of mine said her water pack vest froze up on her, so she couldn’t use it during the race. Like, this was not fun running cold. This was real cold. Did I need to score those extra bad-ass points my first time out? No. Did I, though? I guess so!

The good news is the snow that hit near my house up by the lake was a non-issue to the south, and we got some beautiful (if heatless) sun for the entirety of the race, too. Good for keeping spirits up, at least. And yet, still, it was cold cold.

As for how I did: I did it. If you’ve been following along you know my one goal going into the run was to run the whole way and…I did that. I ran the whole way. I took full advantage of my “I don’t care about pace this time out” mindset. My pace was way at the slowest end of my anticipated range, but I still came in just ahead of my “Something probably went wrong” time. I’ll take it! I definitely went very conservative on pacing, as thoughts of burning out early were strong. And of course the weather this fall was way too kind, affording no appropriate lead-in down to race-day temperatures.

All that said, there really were some nice stretches in the middle where the miles did kind of melt into each other. Five through eight, maybe? Things did get uglier as I went on, though. While I knew for a fact I could do it, I still felt like I had to dig in a bit to convince myself that I was going to do it. That it was going to be way more fun to be able to tell everyone afterward that I did in fact make it. That working for the hard thing, and then achieving it, was exactly the kind of example I wanted to set for my two kids. That if I stopped running, it was going to be a real cold walk back to the finish line…

The course was nice—an out-and-back double-loop along the Cleveland Metroparks parkway drive. Quiet; I did in fact run without music, to prove to myself I could, and without getting into future plans yet, I’m not sure I’m going to feel the need to do that again! Not much in the way of spectators, of course, because of the weather and because of where the course was run. It was fun to see all the fast people running back the other way several times over from my back-of-the-pack vantage point. Huge props to all the volunteers along the course. Every kind word was incredible.

Crossing the finish line, which I finally actually really believed I was going to do once I finally caught sight of it, wasn’t exactly anti-climactic, but it also wasn’t really accompanied by an orchestral swell, either—that feeling, as I said above, really didn’t come until 24 hours later. Crossing the finish line mostly meant oh my god wow I can stop running now. Which felt really nice for a while. Until the cold settled in. Which I knew to anticipate, but, like, I couldn’t really anticipate what standing in freezing temperatures in four sweaty layers was actually going to feel like. Suffice it to say I did not finish my post-race beer—though no cold was going to stop me from having at least a few victory chugs—before I went home, showered with all the hot water in the house, and then collapsed under multiple blankets for a few hours, until my body finally felt almost warm again.

And thus concludes my first half-marathon training cycle, which was as much about the journey as the destination. It’s been a week and I’m feeling surprisingly good. I was concerned about post-race blues, but knock on wood, so far my spirits have stayed up—landing the race right before a busy holiday week probably helped with that. And knowing even before going into the race that it was always going to be part of a bigger picture, with some next steps already tentatively laid out, certainly hasn’t hurt. For now, though, I’ve been out for a couple short, light recovery runs, and I’m feeling okay, and I think it’s going to be nice to just run for fun with no training expectations for a little while, which I haven’t actually done since…April, I think? What a wild year it has been…

When I started writing up these weekly summaries, I wanted to document the downs as much as the ups, and based on my experience training for the 5k in July, I honestly thought there’d be more dramatic downs. Five of those miles came from my easy run on Tuesday, which became an unintentional race pace kind of run for a bit, because I kind of just felt good and the weather kind of just felt perfect and I kind of just went faster than I meant to. It did not suck.

Another five miles came from my actual race pace run on Thursday. In an effort to avoid the “I’m going to go extra sort of fast for the last stretch” thing I’ve been doing the last few weeks, I set my watch to just notify me when the three race pace miles were done, rather than notifying me at each mile. That helped, I think. I mean, it made the race pace segment feel very long, but it stayed pretty steady, which felt nice.

And then of course ten miles came on Saturday morning, which had me out the door before sunrise. I kept the route generally more flat than the previous time I went out for ten miles. It felt long, but it also felt pretty good; certainly better than the week previous. I definitely felt like I needed to push myself to finish, a bit, near the end—ten miles really is so many miles! I think it showed up in my pace graph; my first three miles were on my slower side, but then my last three miles were pretty fast, the last two actually edging into my hypothetical race pace range. All in, where my last three long runs all hit the exact same average pace, this one landed the average at exactly two seconds faster. I’ll take it!

I know, intuitively, that running can be as much a mental game as a physical one. That said, it has long been hard to imagine ever reaching the point where a run could get long enough to become kind of boring. While I’m not exactly there yet, I feel like I can see that territory around me, now, on some of these runs. I’m starting to consciously play with that a bit.

So like, for this long run, instead of having my watch buzz me after every mile, I had it buzz me after every 2.5 miles, to see what it feels like when I thought of the run less as being a collection of ten miles and more as four longer segments. I’m not sure how much of a difference it made, until near the end, when that last segment started, and it felt like, somewhat irrationally, it was going to be so much longer than all the others.

More dramatically, I also took this long run without music. Though I run with music all the time—a subject worth its own post—I’ll leave the headphones behind on race day. Still, thirteen (point one!) miles is a long time to run without one of my primary crutches, and I wanted to take at least one test run ahead of time without music to make sure I actually could do it. Confirmed: I can actually do it! At least, I can for ten miles. I’ll have to trust race day adrenaline will keep me going for the last three, though, because I’m definitely soundtracking next week’s twelve miler. It’s just way more fun that way.

My (current, highly malleable) half-marathon plan 🏃

Aug 29, 2022

I’ve been thinking about trying a half marathon—and, yes, a full marathon—for a while now. Now, I’m actually planning on going after my first half marathon at the Fall Classic in Cleveland on November 22.

Why now? I’ve been running for three years now, pretty consistently for the last year, and I still like it, I want to keep doing it, and I think I’m in a good position to go for it. Coming off the 5k in July I know that having a challenge like that in front of me helps put the day-to-day activity into a framework that helps me keep my interest levels up. Also, I’m 44, and while I know I’ve got plenty of potential running years ahead of me should I choose to accept them, I also know I’m not getting any younger, so if I’ve got even a flicker of interest in it, now’s probably the time to go for it.

I will also say that my goals here are all very soft. I know well that anything from cataclysmic injury to flagging interest in the face of the demanding commitment could derail the whole process and that’s fine. If I make it, I want it to feel incredible, and if I don’t, I want to remember that I’m still running and have run more miles in this lifetime than I may ever have expected I would.

All that said, my ultimate goal, of course, is to tackle a full marathon. I’ve been toying with some plans for a bit now and I think my best bet is start off with a half marathon in November, another in May 2023, and a third in November 2023, before moving on to the full marathon in May 2024. Assuming I’m still healthy! And happy! And that there’s still land to run on that hasn’t been covered by the sea. I think this gives me the chance to build up my confidence and experiment with training approaches and intensities, while still pacing things out in a way so that it stays fun. I don’t need to rush it and make myself miserable, but I don’t want the big goal to be total vaporware.

For this first half marathon, my only goal right now is to run the whole thing without needing walking breaks. I don’t want to set a firm time goal, though I’m guessing I could land it somewhere between 2:15 and 2:30, if I can maintain a healthy pace for the duration. That said, I took walking breaks during my first 5k in 2019, and during my first 10k earlier this year, and even if I’m running like a dream in the weeks leading up to the half marathon, I know there’s a swell chance race-day nerves will kick in and I’ll wind up coming out hot and I’ll need to compensate later. Which will be perfectly fine—all the more reason for giving myself at least three attempts at the half distance before going on to full as I work on figuring out how to tame that tendency.

For my training schedule, I’m going somewhat softly this first time, more softly in some ways than I did for the 5k in July. I’m taking a hybrid of this beginner’s plan and this advanced beginer’s plan. I’m taking the slightly more aggressive cadence of the advanced beginner’s plan for the Tuesday, Thursday, and Saturday runs, but I’m swapping out the Sunday easy run in favor of cross-training, as suggested by the beginner’s plan.

I’m going with the higher-mileage plan just because because I feel like I can do it, and that those few extra miles each week will help me feel more confident going into the race. Generally the miles will be easier miles than I did training for the 5k—like, no hard interval runs, this time out, and my “race pace” runs I think will be less intense than my threshhold runs from the 5k training plan. Mostly I want to continue educating my body about what my “next gear” feels like, to try to get some better sense of what sustainable and potential trouble each feel like. Honestly, pace in general is a huge question mark for me, right now, which is all the more reason to treat this first attempt as an educational experiment.

As for the extra cross-training day on Sundays, while I was running four days a week training for the 5k, while I’m really excited to know I can do that, I kind of want to keep that in reserve for next time. If this first round goes well, introducing the fourth day again next time should be an easy way to help me improve from there. I do also wonder if it was maybe a shade too much, what with including hard intervals and fairly hard threshhold runs in the mix. I’m still working my confidence back up after the mild knee injury I developed during the race and I’m not looking to aggravate that just yet. So aside from just, you know, taking it easy on my joints, I’ve also started getting out on my bicycles again, which has felt really nice, and I’m happy to use cross-training as an excuse to keep putting some easy miles in on the wheels.

All that said, I can’t lie—I’m certainly already second-guessing myself, here. Was it a mistake to tell everyone so early that I’m trying this? Is it a mistakle to try this at all? Is this actually already happening, is this real, am I really doing my first official training runs this week? I guess there’s only one way to answer these questions.

Post race thoughts 🏃

Jul 20, 2022

First of all: I did the thing.

I did the thing!

That’s it. That’s the post-race thought.

As for my thoughts about the thought…

Before this, any time I’ve raced, it’s just been to finish, to cross the finish line. This was the first time I’ve trained and raced with a specific time goal. I’m glad I went through this experiment, and not just because the training pushed me to crush my goal time. I feel like I learned a lot from it, about my range and capabilities as a runner; the effort made me stronger, the edges of my comfort zones pushed back a a couple notches, at least. And of course it kept me on track to keep running through the first hot part of summer.

Of course, ultimately crushing the goal time was like throwing a perfect cherry on top of the sundae…and then throwing an entire second sundae on top of that. All I needed to do to feel succesful was to beat my goal time by at least one second. To come in around three minutes under that time was right up there at the top end of my most optimistic, pie-in-the-sky, head-in-the-cloud secret goals.

My strategy for the run itself was to try to just know that I could do it, that I’d trained hard enough that I could just let my race day adrenaline do its thing. I pictured myself keeping things smooth but swift for the first two or so miles, so that I’d be able to go all-out the last half-mile, but of course, my race day nerves had other plans, and I did almost the opposite of that, with a fast, hard first mile, and a second mile that wasn’t too far off.

After that…I knew I was running fast and hard, but I didn’t really know by how much. (I’d made the conscious choice not to check my watch at all during the run, because I didn’t want to psych myself out mid-race.) Rounding mile two I knew getting to the end was going to be as much a mental game as a physical one (oh, but it was still deeply, intensely physical!). Telling myself that whole last mile that I didn’t need to stop, that it wasn’t time to stop yet, that I didn’t even want to stop, despite all the extremely hard and compelling evidence my body was throwing at me with each breath, was pretty much everything.

Suffice it to say, that planned last half mile kick-up wasn’t in the cards.. I knew I was already leaving everything out there I could, and the only thing left was to dig deep and keep going. Overall, my last mile was my slowest of the three, but the funny thing in retrospect is, is that it was still one of the three fastest complete miles I’ve run maybe ever. “Slowing down” to what was still a ridiculously fast mile, for me? Priceless.

It’s funny to think about now, but at the time, it really felt like time and space were starting todo really weird things that final stretch. That last half mile straight-away up Belle Ave grew longer the further away from Lake Erie I ran. Someone kept moving the train tracks that crossed the mid-point kept moving away from me. The road was in fact rising to meet me…by becoming a mountain directly in front of my face.

And then somewhere past the tracks—actually, it’s been days since the race and I’m still not convinced I’ve reached those tracks yet, but I digress—amid the noise of the street festival past the finish line and the folks cheering for the runners alongside the course—the cheers of strangers are, truly, gifts—there was a guy walking down the sidewalk reading numbers out loud off his phone, twenty-six something, twenty-six something plus one, twenty-six something plus two, and time really did actually slow down for a moment, and I slipped out of my body, and I processed this information, and I realized that I wasn’t just about to reach my goal—this big, dumb, stupid, pointless goal I’d set for myself two months earlier, that I’d been quietly obsessing over for eight weeks—but that I was about to obliterate it, and that realization slammed be back down into my body, and I somehow ran harder.

I may never run like that again. But to feel the something else that lies just below the nothing left briefly take over? To feel that even once was a privilege and a delight. And then going from that to crossing the finish line, running straight into my family—my poor family, having my sweat-drenched, hyperventilating form collapsing all over them—was just perfect.

Running thoughts from someone you shouldn't take advice from 🏃

Jul 11, 2022

I was chatting with a friend of mine about running, and she suggested she might come back to me for training plan advice, which is hilarious because nobody should ask me for advice about anything, ever. That said, that conversation prompted me to think I should probably brain-dump what I’ve learned over the last eight weeks, while it’s top of mind, even if only for my own reference later.

Long story short: I ran my first official 10k in May, at which point I realized I had exactly 8 weeks to go before my next race, a 5k. I’ve been running for about 3 years at this point, though I didn’t get really consistent with it until about last September, after completely bottoming out last summer. Coming out of this stretch of consistency, I was concerned about the summer—I did so well during the winter, would I lose all my progress when the summer heat hit? Having a formal short-term goal on top of a real training plan felt like a good way to keep my motivation and momentum up.

So, I set the goal of not just making it to the 5k, but PRing at it…even if only by one second. Up to this point I’ve mostly tried to look at speed and pace as interesting but not important. I’m too old to need to go fast. My goals this year have been more around consistency and racking up a certain number of miles over the course of the year. But, given how I’ve felt lately and the level consistency I’ve maintained, speed has felt like something interesting I could play with. I’ve felt like I could consciously boost my pace a bit, even if only for one race. So I decided to go for it, knowing once the race is behind me I can happily revert to easier runs to ride out the rest of the summer.

Training plans for running are a dime a dozen. After searching for one intended to help improve a 5k race time, I found this one at Verywell Fit, which felt right, for me, like something I could apply to my current situation with some ease.

I say apply because I had no intention of following any plan to the letter, instead choosing to freely adjust it to meet my needs. I dropped the plan back to 4 runs a week rather than 5; up to that point I was still generally running only 3 times a week, and jumping to 5 felt like a bad idea. I’ve been interested in trying to get an extra run in during the week, though, so it felt like 4 runs a week was a comfortable stretch I was ready to try to make.

I kept what I thought of as the three core runs—the interval run (or, “the one with lots of extremely short, stupidly fast runs”), the tempo run (or, “the one with the mid-length, maintainably swift run”), and the long, slow run (or, “the one with the I-feel-like-I-could-literally-do-this-forever run”)—because I wanted to formally experiment with speed while also consciously slowing down my long run.

A little back story on that last point: leading up to the 10k in May I realized that I was kind of just going out and doing the same run all the time, generally going fast-for-me every time, and while I was getting faster, it also felt unsustainable. I wasn’t playing with all the keys on the piano. Reading around online I kept seeing the same refrain about how most of your training run time should really be lighter, slower, more at a conversational pace. So even before I started the plan I was trying to learn how to slow down more often; with the plan, dedicating a couple runs each week to speed also meant trying my hardest to keep my other runs more low-key, either by pace or heart rate.

All told, all of this pushed me to broaden my sense of my range as a runner—which, yes, calling myself a “runner” makes me feel like a huge imposter, still, sure—to really see what I was capable of at the upper and lower ends of my dials, and to start playing around a bit more with my sense of control and feel. Should I choose to focus on speed, like I plan to do at this race, I have a better sense of what healthy speed feels like and how very bad uh-oh-bad-idea speed can feel, the difference between the pace that can get me through the first two-thirds of the race and the pace I can throw at the last half mile because there’s no reason to hold anything back because I will never ever ever have to run ever again for as long as I live as soon as I hit that finish line (at least until I go out again for my next run sometime after the race). Whatever happens at the race, I’m glad I’ve bettered my sense of feel.

As I said above I did introduce a fourth easy run to my week. With interval runs on Tuesday, tempo runs on Thursday, and my long run on Saturday, I put the easy run on Monday, thinking to give myself a rest day after my long run day (and a free night to enjoy an adult beverage or two on Saturdays, after that long run!). This worked for me, there was a nice rhythm to the week, and I’m hoping after the race to keep the fourth run going, after a couple super easy recovery weeks. I might move it back to Sundays, to the weekend, because holy moly do the weeks feel full right now, but we’ll see.

I liked the rhythm of having at most two runs two days in a row, for me, where I’m at now. There was one week where I did have to move my Monday easy run to Wednesday, and while I did it, while I made three days in a row work that one time, and I actually did pretty well on that Thursday tempo run, I think I still prefer the more steady rate of rest days between run days. It definitely caught up to me in the fays that followed. I’m not there yet, at least not with those fairly intense runs in the mix.

I did little cross-training, because time is hard, though I did try to make sure I walked at least one good solid mile every day to help keep things loose, either as a lunchtime break or just around parks trying to keep up with my two kids. It’s not much, but it’s something. Again, unless the Olympics open up to middle-aged guys who drink beer for fun, what I’m doing right now is just fine, thanks.

I also found a yoga routine that I liked, which occupied Wednesday nights. I’m pretty hooked on that now. The one time I skipped that was the one week where I had some pain that tipped from “I’m an old man and I’m naturally creaky and of course I hurt but it’s, like, a good hurt” over to “Oh no, my body is 75 percent broken-down rusty old cars and 25 percent small woodland creatures making their homes inside those cars.” That week I had to skip my tempo run and shorten my long run; that little bit of recovery space cleared the way for a strong finish, though, letting me land my first (and last, for a while!) 20+ mile week ever just last week.

All in all, the training plan itself went pretty well. I liked making the plan and then following it—doing 90 percent of the thinking up front, mostly, and then just doing the doing after that. For the most part, at least. I definitely felt free to modify details as I went based on what I learned along the way. I learned a bit about when I’m better doing a run by pace instead of heart rate; how to set my watch to give me useful information instead of just constantly screaming at me the whole time that I, a total hobbyist, was unable to consistently keep a completely consistent tempo within a narrow 20 second range; when to go a little harder and when to ease up.

On the flip side…putting all this time toward one specific goal that’s going to happen in a single half-hour timespan early on a single Saturday evening is a weird headspace to be in. That’s the mental game, though. I tell myself that what actually happens at the race doesn’t matter—I could absolutely flub it and have the worst run of my life, and it wouldn’t matter one bit, because the process of getting to that point, the work and improvements I’ve enjoyed leading up to the race, that’s the part that really matters for me.

Which is a very nice thing to tell myself! And it might even be true! Still, though, obviously, of course I’d be lying if I said that reaching that goal, perhaps even crushing it, reaching one of the secret goals I have locked away inside my brain for fear of ruining them by even thinking of speaking them aloud, doesn’t actually matter to me at all. It does. Doing the thing would obviously be the incredibly delightful cherry to drop on top of this sundae. The sundae doesn’t really need it, but it sure would look nice, wouldn’t it?

I’ve got a couple easy runs ahead of me this week, a couple extra rest days, and then it’s race day. My hope is that I’ve both learned a few things that will help me run a better race than I have in the past, and that I’ve also conditioned my system enough so that I comfortably take all my extra race-day adrenaline and pour it all out onto the race course. Whatever happens, it’s not going to matter, but also, I feel pretty good about what could happen.

Once I actually get to do the thing. Can I just do it right now? Get it over with? That’d be nice. No? No? Darn. Worth the ask, anyway.

Summer 2022 running update 🏃

Jun 23, 2022

I started running in 2019 in order to do the MetroHealth Friends of Mothers & Infants Stroll & Roll 5K. The race supports a great cause, and in just a few months, it forced me to go from “Running a full mile would be kind of a neat thing to do!” to “Wow, I just ran three miles! Nobody in the history of the world has ever done that before! I am amazing!”

And then I kept running.

Kind of.

Looking back, I realize my consistency those first two years was very hit or miss. I’m not surprised that it culminated in a full-scale break-down blow-out “This is stupid! I quit! And…and…did I mention this is stupid?” moment last summer. There were other factors at play, sure, but also, it was stupid and running was stupid and I quit.

Turns out, sometimes quitting is smart. I needed to walk away from my running-induced frustrations for a while. After clearing my head, I realized running wasn’t that stupid. And that I wasn’t ready to quit.

So I started back up September with a nice easy 1 mile run.

And then I kept running.

Not every specific run has been fun, obviously, but running itself has been fun in general. I’m not setting world records, I’m not looking to compete against others. But my confidence and enjoyment have both increased.

Races have helped. I did the Stroll & Roll again in the fall, and then I ran a chilly 5k at the Cleveland Fall Classic, and then in the spring I achieved the personal bucket list item of doing a 5k in under 30 minutes at the Treat Yo Self 5k.

Turns out, consistency is good. Who knew! Doing the thing means you can do the thing! And I’m pretty sure I have gotten better at doing the thing, too. Now, I think, from a certain angle, if you squint at the last nine months just right, I think you can safely say that I’ve gone from “very slow but well-meaning” to “kind of actually approaching something resembling thoroughly average”.

Progress!

Most recently I ran the 10k the weekend of the Cleveland Marathon, where I didn’t do as well as I’d hoped I would, and am totally and completely fine with that. What mattered to me more was just doing it at all, getting the experience of it, the education from it, and also oh yeah surprising myself with some really great runs leading up to the race.

And, just, I mean…I did the thing! It’s possible I wore my finisher medal around the house for about a week afterward! Ask my family how much fun and totally not insufferable I am!

Next up, I’m running the Lakewood Summer Meltdown 5k. This race happens in shouting distance of my house and I’ve wanted to do it since I started running. Since even before that: being a bystander at the party in the preceding years, seeing people crossing the finish line, feeling the energy in the air around it all, I think that contributed a lot to me even thinking about starting running.

After I finished the 10k in May I realized I had exactly 8 weeks until the Meltdown 5k. It felt like a good time to experiment with my running schedule and explore different kinds of runs, with the goal of getting my speed up, even just a quarter-notch or so, in order to try to set a new 5k PR at the race. Lo and behold, I found a training plan that catered exactly to that.

I’m about halfway through this 8 week session. I think it’s been going well! I stepped that plan back slightly at the outset—before this I was only running 3 days a week; and 5 felt like it would have been too much of a jump, especially for an old man like me, but 4 felt like a nice, attainable stretch goal, a limit I could get behind pushing. So I’m following the interval run, tempo run, and long run routine, with an extra easy run mixed in there.

This has smoothly but also dramatically pushed my weekly mileage up each week from where I was around the time of the 10k. It’s also varied the kinds of miles I’m running, which has been educational. Consciously doing “easier” miles has felt weird, even for someone who doesn’t really go all that fast when he’s going his fastest, but getting a broader sense of my range, a greater feel for what I can do (run 0.25 mile sprint at a sub-7:30 pace!) and can’t do (run a 0.25 mile sprint at a sub-7:30 pace if I’m going to do more than a few of those sprints in a single session!) has been interesting. It’s fun to know what my hardest-run interval feels like, compared to those easy-breezy I-could-do-this-forever slow-boat long-run miles. Also turns out I absolutely love whatever passes for my version of a tempo run. Cool! That probably means I’m doing it wrong! Bummer!

As for my race goal, if I can shave just one second off my last 5k race time, this will be a total and complete personal victory. And if I don’t, I don’t. But I think I can. So I’m going to go for it.

After that, I’m pondering where I’m going to go from here. There’s a lot of options, now, which is kind of a wild place to be.
