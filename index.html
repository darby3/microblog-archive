I track and share my runs on Strava; on a whim, on my second run this year, I posted, in that run’s notes, the album I listened to during the run (Strange Little Birds by Garbage.) This is a dumb idea. It probably adds more stress to my life than I need. How am I going to track it? How am I going to pick new stuff if I make it to, like, December, or, heck, June? What happens if I use a favorite album too soon, or ruin it on a bad run? Does this actually result in a cool diary of what my year of running sounds like, or does sharing the fact that I do in fact sometimes listen to Taylor Swift absolutely destroy me in the public eye?

Still, it’s the kind of dumb seed of idea that finds fertile ground to develop into a big goofy plant of an idea when I’m out running, when I’m trying my hardest not to think about anything, and now I’m here with a bunch of thoughts and contraints around this absolutely pointless challenge I’m now apparently setting out for myself this year:

  • I’ll continue sharing the album on Strava, and possibly do monthly or quarterly round-up posts here. Until I forget. And then I won’t.
  • Loophole: if I’m craving something truly embarassing, I can redact it from the public records. But, like, as impeccably cool as my first four choices have been, it’ll pretty quickly become clear that, like, my music taste is far from impeccably cool.
  • Once I start the album, that’s it, it’s on the list. Plenty of runs won’t last long enough to cover entire albums. That’s fine. Also the album might just not work and I might have to click over to something else mid-run (see loophole below); the album still goes on the list.
  • Loophole: I’m only going to count the first album of any run. So like on long runs if I queue up multiple albums, anything I listen to after album number one doesn’t count. I’m probably mostly a deliriously unreceptive slate by that point anyway, so it really shouldn’t count.
  • That said, double albums? No two-disc loophole: it’s all one album. (So, yes, I’ll be saving stuff like The Fragile by Nine Inch Nails for seriously long runs.)
  • What happens when I’m running stays when I’m running; anything I listen to outside of running doesn’t count. (Having only recently discovered them, I can safely say I’m going to listen to that Purity Ring album a couple dozen more times this year.)
  • Tracking is by album and not artist. There’s probably going to be a couple discographies I run through. (No pun intended.)
  • Throwing a “liked tracks” list on shuffle is perfectly valid at any point for any reason. And doesn’t count against the list.
  • Same deal for DJ mixes. I have a bucket of these bookmarked on Soundcloud. They’re fun. And they won’t count.
  • Loophole: races don’t count. Historically I haven’t listened to music during races. This year I think I might, and if I do, anything goes; after all, what better time to bring out the big guns?

It’s funny to think about how my relationship to music when I’m running has changed over the last 3.5 years, from my original need to carefully soundtrack every single minute of every single run with custom playlists (it was only mildly high-maintenance…), through to my current of attitude of listening to pretty much literally whatever so long as I like it. Not that I don’t still generally prefer more upbeat stuff when I go out; it’s unlikely I’m about to go out running to Selected Ambient Works Volume II by Aphex Twin anytime soon. But “upbeat”, it seems, can take on a lot of forms, when you have zero thoughts about pace matching or anything like that. (I’d be pretty curious to see what a two-hour run to Autechre’s NTS Session 4 would do to one’s headspace.)

2023 Running Plans üèÉ

Nov 29, 2022

I’m planning on running a full marathon in 2024.

I’ve been planning for this since I started training for the half marathon I just ran. I looked at the half marathon as a chance to dip my toe in the distance-running water to see whether I liked the feel for this kind of training or whether it felt like too much. So far, so good: nothing about the half marathon cycle I just went through made me want to give up.

It’s funny, because even with what I’ve done, I still feel like I have a little imposter syndrome, when it comes to running. I still feel very conservative in my approach, using the word “training” still kind of makes me want to break out in hives, and I come into this knowing that running a full marathon isn’t some huge life-long dream of mine. (For that, you’d have to look at biking. While I’ve biked one metric century, I still feel like I’ve got at least one full 100 mile ride in me. Some day.) I started running three years ago in order to support a particular charity 5k, and with two kids in the house I was looking for a form of exercise that was a bit more effective than bicycling (which long rides, when I was training for that metric century, took me out of the house forrrevvvver), and because I generally thought it would be cool to be able to run a full mile without stopping. After a couple rocky years I got more consistent with running last year and started wondering if I could run a marathon.

And I started to think that maybe I could. And I started to think that maybe if I thought I could, if I even had an inkling of interest in it, maybe I should do something about it sooner rather than later, because while I’m not old, I’m certainly not getting any younger. At one point I thought about just going straight for it in 2023 but then decided I’m just not that hardcore. So I came up with a loose plan to give myself well over a year to build up to it while also giving myself plenty of freedom to back out at any time for any reason with no penalty.

And now here I am, my first half marathon in the books, and as of right now, that big-picture goal still seems reasonable. I mean, it seems crazy, but also possible. Nothing about this process the last few months made me want to quit. Whether or not I actually make it all the way I feel like I’m having fun right now, like I’m building up a sustainable running practice, and I’m learning a lot about what motivates me to keep going, and of which is worth it.

And I can still run one whole mile without stopping. And that still does feel pretty cool!

That said, I’ve learned a bit about how goal motivated I am. Races‚Äîas much as I anguish over them‚Äîbring structure and focus to all the runs that come before them. So I’m going to keep doing them, and I’m planning on doing two more half-marathons in 2023. I want to re-run the Fall Classic in November, because I want to have at least one year-over-year apples-to-apples comparison, just to see what there is to see. Before that I want to try for a late spring race, I think, because I’d like to run at least one of these races in (hopefully) nice weather. I’ve earned that!

I’m also ready to care about pace a little more. Admittedly, I ran the November race slow enough that it would be tough not to beat that time next time out, after another round of training, but also I just know I can go faster than that, and I want to specifically try for that. I never expect to be competitive at these things, nor am I interested in trying hard enough to be. But I also know it’s fun to surprise yourself, and I can get behind trying hard enough for that. So I’m setting out with the loose goal this spring of beating my first time, and then going for something reasonably ambitious in the fall.

To that end, in the short term, I want to run more during the next training cycle. Not necessarily harder, but more. I know I trained at about the barest possible minimal level for the fall race. Next time out I want to go from three days a week to four. I think I’m going to repeat the same basic 12-week training calendar as the last time out, which I liked the general feel of, with the addition of the fourth day, which would be a shorter, light/easy day. I have no plans to target five days as I want to keep a day or two free for an elliptical session or finding a strength training or yoga habit I like.

While I said I don’t necessarily want to run harder, I do generally want to bring my paces up across the board. Sometimes these last few months I felt fine, sometimes I felt like I had more to give but was holding back. I’m still not quite sure what easy should actually feel like but I think just in general the more I run the more efficient I get and if I can maintain my consistency through into the new year I’ll naturally feel a little more okay going a little more fast a little more often. Maybe more importantly though, for confidence purposes, I want to push my race pace experiments more aggressively. I want to pick an ambitious target early and stick with it the whole way through. I really wasn’t sure what felt right this last time through, and now I think I want to use those runs to push myself to give a little more. Still, even if it’s nothing like what I run on race day, having those faster days in the training cycle feels fun.

I’m also hoping to do some shorter races that I can really try for my best-possible paces. I’m thinking about re-doing the 10k I ran last spring, which left me feeling like I had something unresolved there that I’d like to make good on. I’m also curious to see if I can beat my summer 5k race’s time from last year, too. Which actually feels like it could be the most ambitious goal of the year! If I find some 10k races that fit in with the half marathon plans, I might try to slide those in, too, for practice purposes, but I’m not sure that’s going to work out or not.

So that’s the year in a nutshell, as I picture it right now: a half, some shorter stuff, and then a give-it-my-all half by the year’s end. Working on confidence, pace, and strength. And then all going well, launching into full marathon training come January 2024.

To back up a bit: the week before the race I continued my taper. It got a little weird! I felt good Friday, and slept like a baby that night, which was nice, because I knew I’d probably not sleep like a baby the night before the race. (Spoiler alert: I did not sleep good Saturday night.) Saturday felt like one of the longest days of my life. I’m learning just how susceptible I am to pre-race nerves. I know it’s healthy to a degree, the body’s way of getting amped up for the coming event. But I also think I was tipping a bit past “excitement” and more into “bro, you must chill” territory. Of course, ultimately crushing the goal time was like throwing a perfect cherry on top of the sundae…and then throwing an entire second sundae on top of that. All I needed to do to feel succesful was to beat my goal time by at least one second. My strategy for the run itself was to try to just know that I could do it, that I‚Äôd trained hard enough that I could just let my race day adrenaline do its thing. After that…I knew I was running fast and hard, but I didn’t really know by how much. (I’d made the conscious choice not to check my watch at all during the run, because I didn’t want to psych myself out mid-race.) Overall, my last mile was my slowest of the three, but the funny thing in retrospect is, is that it was still one of the three fastest complete miles I’ve run maybe ever. “Slowing down” to what was still a ridiculously fast mile, for me? It’s funny to think about now, but at the time, it really felt like time and space were starting todo really weird things that final stretch. Running thoughts from someone you shouldn't take advice from üèÉ

Jul 11, 2022

I was chatting with a friend of mine about running, and she suggested she might come back to me for training plan advice, which is hilarious because nobody should ask me for advice about anything, ever. That said, that conversation prompted me to think I should probably brain-dump what I’ve learned over the last eight weeks, while it’s top of mind, even if only for my own reference later.

Long story short: I ran my first official 10k in May, at which point I realized I had exactly 8 weeks to go before my next race, a 5k. I’ve been running for about 3 years at this point, though I didn’t get really consistent with it until about last September, after completely bottoming out last summer. Coming out of this stretch of consistency, I was concerned about the summer‚ÄîI did so well during the winter, would I lose all my progress when the summer heat hit? Having a formal short-term goal on top of a real training plan felt like a good way to keep my motivation and momentum up.

So, I set the goal of not just making it to the 5k, but PRing at it…even if only by one second. Up to this point I’ve mostly tried to look at speed and pace as interesting but not important. I’m too old to need to go fast. My goals this year have been more around consistency and racking up a certain number of miles over the course of the year. But, given how I’ve felt lately and the level consistency I’ve maintained, speed has felt like something interesting I could play with. I’ve felt like I could consciously boost my pace a bit, even if only for one race. So I decided to go for it, knowing once the race is behind me I can happily revert to easier runs to ride out the rest of the summer.

Training plans for running are a dime a dozen. After searching for one intended to help improve a 5k race time, I found this one at Verywell Fit, which felt right, for me, like something I could apply to my current situation with some ease.

I say apply because I had no intention of following any plan to the letter, instead choosing to freely adjust it to meet my needs. I dropped the plan back to 4 runs a week rather than 5; up to that point I was still generally running only 3 times a week, and jumping to 5 felt like a bad idea. I’ve been interested in trying to get an extra run in during the week, though, so it felt like 4 runs a week was a comfortable stretch I was ready to try to make.

I kept what I thought of as the three core runs‚Äîthe interval run (or, “the one with lots of extremely short, stupidly fast runs”), the tempo run (or, “the one with the mid-length, maintainably swift run”), and the long, slow run (or, “the one with the I-feel-like-I-could-literally-do-this-forever run”)‚Äîbecause I wanted to formally experiment with speed while also consciously slowing down my long run.

A little back story on that last point: leading up to the 10k in May I realized that I was kind of just going out and doing the same run all the time, generally going fast-for-me every time, and while I was getting faster, it also felt unsustainable. I wasn’t playing with all the keys on the piano. Reading around online I kept seeing the same refrain about how most of your training run time should really be lighter, slower, more at a conversational pace. So even before I started the plan I was trying to learn how to slow down more often; with the plan, dedicating a couple runs each week to speed also meant trying my hardest to keep my other runs more low-key, either by pace or heart rate.

All told, all of this pushed me to broaden my sense of my range as a runner‚Äîwhich, yes, calling myself a “runner” makes me feel like a huge imposter, still, sure‚Äîto really see what I was capable of at the upper and lower ends of my dials, and to start playing around a bit more with my sense of control and feel. Should I choose to focus on speed, like I plan to do at this race, I have a better sense of what healthy speed feels like and how very bad uh-oh-bad-idea speed can feel, the difference between the pace that can get me through the first two-thirds of the race and the pace I can throw at the last half mile because there’s no reason to hold anything back because I will never ever ever have to run ever again for as long as I live as soon as I hit that finish line (at least until I go out again for my next run sometime after the race). Whatever happens at the race, I’m glad I’ve bettered my sense of feel.

As I said above I did introduce a fourth easy run to my week. With interval runs on Tuesday, tempo runs on Thursday, and my long run on Saturday, I put the easy run on Monday, thinking to give myself a rest day after my long run day (and a free night to enjoy an adult beverage or two on Saturdays, after that long run!). This worked for me, there was a nice rhythm to the week, and I’m hoping after the race to keep the fourth run going, after a couple super easy recovery weeks. I might move it back to Sundays, to the weekend, because holy moly do the weeks feel full right now, but we’ll see.

I liked the rhythm of having at most two runs two days in a row, for me, where I’m at now. There was one week where I did have to move my Monday easy run to Wednesday, and while I did it, while I made three days in a row work that one time, and I actually did pretty well on that Thursday tempo run, I think I still prefer the more steady rate of rest days between run days. It definitely caught up to me in the fays that followed. I’m not there yet, at least not with those fairly intense runs in the mix.

I did little cross-training, because time is hard, though I did try to make sure I walked at least one good solid mile every day to help keep things loose, either as a lunchtime break or just around parks trying to keep up with my two kids. It’s not much, but it’s something. Again, unless the Olympics open up to middle-aged guys who drink beer for fun, what I’m doing right now is just fine, thanks.

I also found a yoga routine that I liked, which occupied Wednesday nights. I’m pretty hooked on that now. The one time I skipped that was the one week where I had some pain that tipped from “I’m an old man and I’m naturally creaky and of course I hurt but it’s, like, a good hurt” over to “Oh no, my body is 75 percent broken-down rusty old cars and 25 percent small woodland creatures making their homes inside those cars.” That week I had to skip my tempo run and shorten my long run; that little bit of recovery space cleared the way for a strong finish, though, letting me land my first (and last, for a while!) 20+ mile week ever just last week.

All in all, the training plan itself went pretty well. I liked making the plan and then following it‚Äîdoing 90 percent of the thinking up front, mostly, and then just doing the doing after that. For the most part, at least. I definitely felt free to modify details as I went based on what I learned along the way. I learned a bit about when I’m better doing a run by pace instead of heart rate; how to set my watch to give me useful information instead of just constantly screaming at me the whole time that I, a total hobbyist, was unable to consistently keep a completely consistent tempo within a narrow 20 second range; when to go a little harder and when to ease up.

On the flip side…putting all this time toward one specific goal that’s going to happen in a single half-hour timespan early on a single Saturday evening is a weird headspace to be in. That’s the mental game, though. I tell myself that what actually happens at the race doesn’t matter‚ÄîI could absolutely flub it and have the worst run of my life, and it wouldn’t matter one bit, because the process of getting to that point, the work and improvements I’ve enjoyed leading up to the race, that’s the part that really matters for me.

Which is a very nice thing to tell myself! And it might even be true! Still, though, obviously, of course I’d be lying if I said that reaching that goal, perhaps even crushing it, reaching one of the secret goals I have locked away inside my brain for fear of ruining them by even thinking of speaking them aloud, doesn’t actually matter to me at all. It does. Doing the thing would obviously be the incredibly delightful cherry to drop on top of this sundae. The sundae doesn’t really need it, but it sure would look nice, wouldn’t it?

I’ve got a couple easy runs ahead of me this week, a couple extra rest days, and then it’s race day. My hope is that I’ve both learned a few things that will help me run a better race than I have in the past, and that I’ve also conditioned my system enough so that I comfortably take all my extra race-day adrenaline and pour it all out onto the race course. Whatever happens, it’s not going to matter, but also, I feel pretty good about what could happen.

Once I actually get to do the thing. Can I just do it right now? Get it over with? That’d be nice. No? No? Darn. Worth the ask, anyway.
